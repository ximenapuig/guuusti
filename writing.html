as a FASHION DIRECTOR

"COPYCATS"
It was the day after Virgil Abloh’s 2021 Spring/Summer “Zoom with Friends” collection for Louis Vuitton’s menswear line; other than the actual day of the show, the toughest day as a fashion designer. August 7th was the day critics, the press and industry experts met on media platforms to critique the top luxury houses’ new collection. However, this time around, Abloh was receiving the most negative feedback he had collected since becoming a fashion designer. Walter Van Beirendonck, the head of fashion at the Royal Academy of Fine Arts in Antwerp , an extremely notable designer, had took to his Instagram account to share his thoughts on the collection. Abloh was quick to panic after seeing Van Beirendonck’s Instagram post depicting Louis Vuitton’s collections awful similarity to his (Figure 1). Abloh stressed about similar past critiques knowing that this could take his position as artistic director of menswear for Louis Vuitton…
Virgil Abloh
Abloh is an entrepreneur born and raised on the 30th of September in the outskirts of Chicago. Abloh attended the University of Wisconsin to pursue an undergraduate degree in civil engineering. Shortly after, Abloh completed his master’s degree in architecture at the Illinois Institute of Technology. Abloh credited architecture for his quickly growing interest in fashion. In 2009, Kanye West and Virgil Abloh became interns at Fendi in Rome. Since, they continued working together on Kanye’s brand until 2012 when Abloh launched his first brand, Pyrex Vision. Under Pyrex Vision, Abloh would buy new Ralph Lauren flannel shirts for $40 each, and screen print Pyrex with the number 23 under it in a jersey style way on them; he would sell them for $550 each. In 2013, Abloh stopped Pyrex and started Off-White, a high fashion brand which would soon become the second “hottest brand” (Quarter 2 of 2020) according to the Lyst Index, one of the most important indexes in fashion. While working on Off-White, Abloh created partnerships with brands like Nike, Ikea, Jimmy Choo, Golden Goose Deluxe Brand and Warby
Louis Vuitton
In 1854, Louis Vuitton, a trunk maker in Paris established his own eponymous brand which quickly became a high-class status symbol. At the time, horse-drawn carriages, boats and train were the main modes of transportation, yet finding where to take valuables was always a problem. Louis Vuitton sold hand-crafted luxury travel trunks to solve this problem. In 1896, four years after Louis Vuitton’s death, the company introduced the first monogram print, the print which would lead Louis Vuitton to the top luxury brand in the later 1900s. In 1987, Louis Vuitton became LVMH (Louis Vuitton Moet and Hennessey) to form the largest luxury goods conglomerate . Since then, Louis Vuitton has expanded into ready-to-wear clothing, accessories such as handbags, wallets and luggage’s as well as luxury household goods while rotating the designers in charge of these collections. In 2018, Abloh was handed the title of Artistic Director for Menswear by his dear friend Kim Jones who left Louis Vuitton to design at Dior. LVMH now owns 75 brands which all share LVMHs’ values and purpose of cultivating an entrepreneurial spirit by being creative and innovative all while delivering excellence.
3% Rule
At a guest lecture at Harvard University, Abloh explains how changing anything by 3% makes it an entirely new item (an example can be seen in figure 2). Moreover, Abloh completes just 70% of his projects before moving on to his next project, allowing him to work on a million projects at once. Consequently, Abloh’s purpose and products are often criticized due to their similarity to other designer work. In an interview with GQ, Raf Simons, a veteran designer and member of the Antwerp Six, was asked if he was inspired by young designers like Virgil Abloh and their ability to connect with the youth. Raf responded “I’m inspired by people who bring something that I think has not been seen, that is original”. He then expanded by talking about Abloh’s work ethic (3% rule) and the appropriation towards other designers, including himself (Figure 3) and designers such as Gramm and Colrs, A.G Fronzoni, Ryder Rips.
Walter Van Beirendonck
Van Beirendonck is a Belgian born designer who also forms a member of the Antwerp Six and is now the head of fashion at their alma mater, the Royal Academy of Antwerp. In the 1980’s, Van Beirendonck attended the Royal Academy alongside Martin Margiela, Dries Van Noten, Ann Demeulemeester, Dirk Bikkembergs and Raf Simons. This set of designers, the Antwerp Six, has been named the most influential designers in history as the arguably played a huge role in shaping modern day fashion. In 1983, Van Beirendonck established his eponymous brand. Since, he been known for his daring, out of the box collections with notable cuts, colors and prints.
Virgil Abloh and Walter Van Beirendonck
On August 7th, the day after Abloh’s 2021 Spring/Summer Collection, Van Beirendonck posted an image of a shirt he had previously designed that read “I HATE FASHION COPYCATS”. He then posted a photo showing the obvious similarity in aesthetic, silhouettes, color palette and overall motif of the stuffed animals on the garments (Figure 1). The similarity between the eyewear for Abloh’s collection and eyewear that Van Beirendonck had done in the past (Figure 4) was later addressed as well. “Virgil did it again,” Van Beirendonck told HYPEBEAST soon after the posts. “This is not just copying, this is using my world, ideas, colors, signatures, cuts, shapes, as his collection mood board. A world and signature [I’ve been] working on since 1985. He is paid a huge amount of money to be an [‘artistic director’] and he has unlimited possibilities to work with anybody in the world – he could have asked me for a collaboration”.
Appropriation vs. Homage
An homage to an artist is a celebration, even elevation of their work by giving them credence and understanding . On the other hand, appropriation is when a person, artist or business takes a piece of culture, art, or of someone’s ideas and then leverages it for their own benefit. This is usually seen from people in a privileged position taking from a person or group from an unprivileged position and then leveraging their art without any appreciation or understanding of the context or its significance . Who is more privileged at this point? Walter Van Beirendonck is a member of the Antwerp Six, the most revered fashion designers of the 20th century. Despite this, their influence fades off outside their niche circle of supporters. Van Beirendonck’s work has only been carried in major department stores through few brand partnerships and collaborations giving him little exposure. On the other hand, Virgil Abloh is one of the most well- known and prominent designers in the world today; he holds a position at Louis Vuitton, the largest luxury brand. He is arguably much better paid than Van Beirendonck and has much more influence outside of the industry. It seems Abloh is in the position of privilege however it is important to note that Van Beirendonck is in the traditional position of privilege as he is a white, European man who studied, and now works at at the best school for his industry. Abloh, on the other hand, is an inner city, black, Chicago entrepreneur that did not study fashion or work in fashion prior to starting his own brand.
Abloh stresses about what his team, the CEO Michael Burk, and the head of LVMH Bernard Arnault were going to say. Has his 3% rule become a pass for appropriating others work? How has Abloh reflected Louis Vuitton’s purpose of driving entrepreneurship and creativity? How will this affect the conglomerates (LVMH) brand image and consequently, brand partnerships?
